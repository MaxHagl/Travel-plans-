Why this one

Two valleys that shouldn't be this quiet

The Tannheimer Tal sits at 1,100 metres in the Tyrolean limestone Alps, just south of the German border and almost completely unknown outside of Austrian and Bavarian hiking circles. It's a high valley of unusual width and stillness: three glacially-carved lakes strung along the valley floor, ringed by pale limestone peaks, and — on the eastern approach road — technically closed to private cars after 8 a.m. in summer. The car ban, which protects the Vilsalpsee nature reserve, is the accidental secret of the valley: it keeps the whole eastern end quiet and forces visitors to slow down.

The marquee day combines two of the valley's finest features: the lake-to-lake hike from the Vilsalpsee up to the Traualpsee and the Obere Traualpe, a working alpine farm at 1,540 metres with the Lachenspitze towering above it; and in the afternoon, the Gaichtpass, a small, genuinely scenic pass road that tips you into the Lechtal — a valley so committed to being left alone that the Lech river here still braids freely across open gravel beds, one of the last wild glacial rivers in the entire Alpine chain.

The second night in Holzgau gives you the suspension bridge — 200 metres long, 110 metres above the Höhenbachtal gorge, still swaying slightly when there's no wind — and the Simms waterfall in the morning before the drive home. It is a more varied and visually eventful trip than its modest hike grade suggests.

Where you sleep

Two nights, two valleys

Night 1 · Grän / Tannheim

By the Haldensee

The Haldensee sits in Grän — the valley's quieter western end — and the small cluster of hotels and family Gasthöfe here have the lake outside the window and the Neunerköpfle cable car five minutes' drive away. Water temperature reaches 21–23°C in mid-July and the lake has a small public swimming area. The vibe is relaxed and genuinely low-key for a resort village.

~€140–180 / night · double · breakfast included
Night 2 · Holzgau

In the Lechtal, under the bridge

Holzgau is a proper small Tyrolean village — around 400 people, a handful of accommodation options, and the valley's most dramatic walk 30 minutes from the door. The Gasthof Bergmahd is the main traditional option; there are also a couple of well-run family pensions. Rooms fill early because the village is small: book this one first when planning.

~€130–165 / night · double · breakfast included

Book Holzgau particularly early — very limited rooms. Both hotels 8–10 weeks out for mid-July.

Day by day

Three days in the route

Day 1 · Arrival

Into the valley & the lake at sunset

From Ingolstadt head south on the A7 to Kempten, then southeast via Wertach into the Tannheimer Tal — the last stretch of road descends into the valley with the Haldensee suddenly below you, blue and still in the afternoon light. Check in at your Grän hotel by early afternoon.

The Haldensee is the valley's social lake: a flat, easy 3 km path circles it, the swimming area on the east shore is warm by July, and pedal boats are available if you'd rather be on the water than beside it. In the evening, the Neunerköpfle cable car from Tannheim (5 minutes' drive) runs until around 6 p.m. and rises to just under 1,900 metres — the panorama from the top stretches from the valley below, past the Zugspitze to the east, to the main Tyrolean ridge to the south. The sun leaves the peaks long after it leaves the valley floor. Dinner back in Tannheim or Grän.

Switch hotels after the hike

After descending from the Obere Traualpe, the drive over the Gaichtpass to Holzgau takes around 45 minutes — a genuinely scenic crossing through open hill country above the treeline. Two different worlds separated by a single low pass.

Day 2 · The big day

Lakes in layers, then over the pass

The Vilsalpsee road closes to private cars from 8 a.m. to 5 p.m. to protect the nature reserve. Either arrive before 8 — easy to do if you're staying in the valley — or park in Tannheim and ride the Alpenexpress shuttle (roughly €7–8 return, runs frequently on summer mornings). The lake is a 1.5 km flat walk from the car park along the western shore, where the path follows the water under the limestone cliffs of the Lachenspitze.

At the far end of the lake the trail climbs through mixed forest to the Traualpsee (roughly 2 hours from the car park, ~400 m ascent) — a smaller, quieter, higher lake set in a tighter horseshoe of rock. Continue for another 30–40 minutes up to the Obere Traualpe, the working alp at 1,540 m that marks the end of the marked route. The view back down through both lakes to the valley floor is the payoff; there's usually food and drinks at the alm. Descend the same way — the western shore path back to the car park takes about 90 minutes.

Drive over the Gaichtpass in the afternoon. The crossing itself is worth taking slowly — there are several pull-outs with long views down into the Lechtal below. Check in to your Holzgau hotel, then a short evening walk to see the suspension bridge: the gorge is already dramatic in the low light, and the bridge is lit at night.

Day 3 · Morning & home

Across the bridge, past the waterfall, onto the road

The Holzgau suspension bridge circuit is a morning well spent: 30 minutes on foot from the village to the bridge, then across the 200-metre span to the far side of the Höhenbachtal gorge, following the gorge path south to the Simms waterfall — a 30-metre cascade that an English engineer blasted into existence in the 1870s to give the route a dramatic final note — and on to Café Uta at the Rossgumpenalm for coffee with the valley spread below you. The full circuit back to Holzgau takes around 2 hours. Lunch in the village, then home via Reutte and the Fernpass to the A7, arriving in Ingolstadt in approximately 3 hours.

The marquee hike

Vilsalpsee · Traualpsee · Obere Traualpe

Trail profile

Start1,100 m
Top1,540 m
Ascent~500 m
Distance~10–12 km
Time3–3.5 hours
GradeT2 · Moderate

The route starts at the Vilsalpsee car park — arrive before 8 a.m. and you'll have the road to yourselves, the valley quiet and the light low on the cliffs. The path runs along the western shore of the lake, flat and easy for the first two kilometres; the Lachenspitze reflects off the water on the right. At the far end of the lake the trail turns upward, climbing through beech and spruce forest on a steady, well-marked path. There are no exposed sections and no technical difficulties — just consistent uphill on a good mountain trail.

The Traualpsee appears without warning through a gap in the trees at around 1,300 metres — a smaller, greener lake set in a tighter bowl of limestone, usually with only a handful of other walkers. From here the path continues up to the Obere Traualpe through open alm terrain. The working farm at 1,540 metres is the real destination: a bench in the sun, the Lachenspitze and Rauhhorn filling the skyline, and — if the owners are around — the possibility of milk, cheese, and something baked. Descend the same way; the views back down through both lakes to the valley floor get better as the light drops in the afternoon.

The eastern shore trail that would normally complete a loop was closed due to rockfall from the Lachenspitze face; check current conditions locally before the trip in case it has reopened. Even as an out-and-back, the route loses nothing.

Arrive before 8 a.m. or take the Alpenexpress shuttle from Tannheim (runs frequently, ~€7–8 return). The road to the Vilsalpsee is closed to private cars from 8 a.m. to 5 p.m. — this is enforced.
The car park at the Vilsalpsee trailhead is small and fills before the road closes. Before-8-a.m. arrival solves both the parking and the access question simultaneously.
July afternoon thunderstorms are less severe here than in the higher Berchtesgaden Alps, but the exposed upper alm at 1,540 m is still not ideal in lightning. Starting by 9 a.m. at the latest keeps you comfortably down before the usual 2–3 p.m. storm window.
Boots with ankle support are recommended; trail runners are adequate on this trail in dry conditions but can slip on wet limestone. The route is well-marked throughout.
The Vilsalpsee nature reserve has strict no-swimming rules — the lake looks inviting and the water is clear, but swimming and any off-path access are prohibited. Save the swimming for the Haldensee on Day 1.

Before you go

Practical notes

🚗

Getting there

Ingolstadt → Tannheimer Tal is approximately 230 km, primarily on the A7 to Kempten then southeast via Wertach and the Austrian border. An Austrian motorway vignette (€12.40 / 10 days) is required for any section of Austrian motorway. The return home via Reutte and the Fernpass uses regional roads.

🚌

Vilsalpsee access

The road to the Vilsalpsee is closed to private cars from 8 a.m. to 5 p.m. to protect the nature reserve — this is enforced. Arrive before 8 a.m. (easy if staying in the valley) or park in Tannheim and take the Alpenexpress shuttle (runs every 30–45 min on summer mornings, ~€7–8 return).

🚡

Neunerköpfle cable car

The Neunerköpfle Bahn runs from Tannheim to the summit station at ~1,895 m. Standard summer operating hours are roughly 9 a.m. to 5:30 p.m., with last ascent around 5 p.m. Ticket prices run around €15–18 per person return. Perfect for Day 1's golden-hour panorama.

🌊

Swimming

The Haldensee in Grän reaches 21–23°C by mid-July and has a proper Freibad (outdoor swimming area) on the east shore. The Vilsalpsee, by contrast, has swimming prohibited throughout the nature reserve — the lake and its banks are strictly protected. Save the swim for Day 1.

📅

Book Holzgau first

Holzgau has very limited accommodation — perhaps 30–50 guest beds in total across all options. It books out faster than Tannheim. Lock in your Holzgau night before searching for the Grän/Tannheim hotel. Both need to be booked 8–10 weeks ahead for mid-July.

🌉

The suspension bridge

The Holzgau suspension bridge circuit (to the bridge, across, along the gorge to the Simms waterfall, then to Café Uta) takes about 2 hours at an easy pace. The bridge itself is free to cross and open year-round (subject to weather). It's lit at night — worth the 10-minute evening walk from the village just to see the gorge lit up.
